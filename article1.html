The ₹2 Crore Mistake: Why Indian R&D Centres Keep Getting the First Hire Wrong

When a global engineering firm decides to build a greenfield R&D centre in India, the most critical decision is not the location or the tax structure. It is the first hire — the founding site leader. Get this wrong, and the centre will burn cash for two years before delivering a single viable product.

The standard playbook for multinational corporations entering Bangalore or Pune is highly predictable. The global board signs off on the India strategy, real estate consultants are engaged to secure Grade A office space, and an international search firm is retained to find the local leader.

Months pass. The search firm produces candidates who look exceptional on paper — usually senior directors from massive, established Indian IT services firms. Interviews happen, an offer is made, and the new leader arrives.

Six months later, the parent company realises the mistake. The new leader knows how to manage a 5,000-person mature operation with established processes, but they have absolutely no idea how to stand in an empty room and build an engineering team from scratch. They are an operator, not a builder.

The Expatriate Default

Faced with a stalled greenfield site and mounting pressure from the board, the parent company panics and hits the default button: they depute a trusted expatriate leader from headquarters to take over.

This is the ₹2 Crore Mistake.

An expatriate deputation at the VP or Director level, including housing allowances, international schooling, tax equalisation, and hardship premiums, routinely costs upwards of ₹2 crore (approx. $250,000 USD) annually. More importantly, the expatriate usually lacks the local network required to actually hire the technical bench.

The Builder vs The Operator

The failure of the initial local search is almost always a failure of assessment. Large search firms look for keyword matches and brand names. They see “VP Engineering at [Major IT Firm]” and assume competence.

But greenfield leadership requires a completely different psychological profile. A greenfield leader must be comfortable with ambiguity. They must be willing to personally interview the first 50 engineers. They must know how to navigate local regulatory setup while simultaneously arguing technical architecture with global counterparts.

At SevenSeers, when we run a greenfield leadership mandate, we do not look for the person running the largest team. We look for the person who built a team five years ago and is now bored with maintaining it. We look for the builder.

That is how we closed the founding leadership mandate for ABB’s Corporate Research Centre in one month, on a single CV, at less than half the cost of the planned expatriate deputation. The difference is not in the size of the database; it is in knowing exactly what a builder looks like.
